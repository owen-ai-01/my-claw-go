Managed hosting sounds like the lazy option. You pay someone to do something you could theoretically do yourself, and you get less control in exchange.

I thought so too — until I started actually tracking how much time I spent on my self-hosted OpenClaw setup over three months. Setup was about four hours. A Node.js version conflict during an OpenClaw update ate another two. A certificate renewal issue took 45 minutes. A few smaller things added up to maybe two hours more.

That's roughly eight hours over a quarter for a service that's supposed to be running in the background doing things for me.

This isn't a hit piece on self-hosting. For some use cases it's clearly the right call. But the calculation is less obvious than it appears when you're just comparing dollar amounts.

---

## The Real Cost of Self-Hosting OpenClaw

The financial math is straightforward: a VPS capable of running OpenClaw comfortably costs $5–$20/month. Managed OpenClaw hosting starts around $16–$30/month. On pure dollar cost, self-hosting wins.

But self-hosting has a time cost that doesn't show up on the invoice:

**Initial setup (2–6 hours).** Provisioning a VPS, installing Node.js, configuring OpenClaw, setting up a systemd service, connecting API keys. The OpenClaw documentation is decent, but there are enough configuration steps that something almost always requires troubleshooting.

**Updates (variable).** OpenClaw updates regularly. Most updates are smooth — `git pull`, restart the service. Some aren't. Dependency changes, configuration format changes, and the occasional breaking change in upstream AI provider APIs can turn a routine update into an hour of debugging.

**Troubleshooting (unpredictable).** Services crash. VPSes go offline for maintenance. SSL certificates expire. When your self-hosted OpenClaw setup breaks on a Wednesday afternoon, that's your problem to solve.

**Monitoring (ongoing).** If you want to know when your OpenClaw instance is down, you need to set that up yourself. Most people don't, which means they discover their instance was unreachable when they try to use it.

None of this is insurmountable. But "self-hosting is just a $5/month VPS" undersells the ongoing time commitment.

## What Managed OpenClaw Hosting Actually Provides

The obvious thing: you don't have to do any of the above. No initial setup, no updates to manage, no troubleshooting when the service goes down.

But there are some less obvious things worth knowing:

**Your instance is always at the latest stable version.** When OpenClaw releases a fix or a new capability, managed hosting providers update their infrastructure. You get the improvement without doing anything.

**Uptime monitoring is built in.** The provider is watching their servers. If your instance goes down, they know before you do.

**API key management.** Some managed OpenClaw hosting services — including MyClawGo — provision a dedicated OpenRouter API key per user. You get AI model access without having to manage provider accounts yourself.

**Backup and persistence.** MyClawGo backs up your agent configurations, documents, and group setups to a database. If something happens to the underlying server, your workspace can be restored. With self-hosting, you're responsible for your own backups.

## What Self-Hosting Is Still Better For

I want to be clear here: self-hosting OpenClaw is not wrong. There are real reasons to choose it.

**Full control.** You decide what version of OpenClaw runs. You decide what tools are available to your agents. You decide how the network is configured. No managed service gives you that level of control.

**Data residency.** If your data needs to stay in a specific geographic region, or can't leave your own infrastructure, self-hosting is the only option. Managed services can tell you where their servers are, but you're still trusting a third party.

**Cost at scale.** If you're running multiple OpenClaw instances for a team, the per-user cost of managed hosting adds up. At some point, operating your own infrastructure becomes more economical even accounting for the time cost.

**Development and customization.** If you're building on top of OpenClaw — writing custom tools, modifying the gateway, testing unreleased features — you need direct access to the environment. Managed hosting is a black box by design.

## How to Decide

The question isn't really "which is better" — it's "what do I actually need?"

**Choose self-hosted OpenClaw if:**  
You have Linux experience and time to invest in setup. Your use case requires full control or data residency guarantees. You're running at a scale where managed hosting costs become significant. You want to develop or customize OpenClaw.

**Choose managed OpenClaw hosting if:**  
You want to use OpenClaw, not maintain it. You're a solo user or small team without dedicated infrastructure time. Your time is more expensive than the price difference between a VPS and a hosting plan. You want a reliable, always-on instance without thinking about it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I migrate from self-hosted OpenClaw to managed hosting?**  
Yes. Your agent configurations and system prompts are easy to recreate. Conversation history and memory don't migrate automatically, but the setup for your actual agents (names, models, instructions) can be copied quickly.

**Can I migrate from managed hosting back to self-hosted?**  
Also yes. You're not locked in to a managed service. Your agent configurations are standard OpenClaw format, and you can recreate them on a self-hosted instance at any time.

**How much does self-hosting OpenClaw actually cost per month?**  
A minimum viable VPS for OpenClaw (2 CPU, 4GB RAM) runs about $7–15/month from providers like Hetzner or DigitalOcean. Add the cost of AI API calls (paid separately, not included in VPS cost), and total monthly costs depend heavily on how much you use it.

**Is managed OpenClaw hosting secure?**  
It depends on the provider. The relevant question is whether you get a dedicated instance (your data on a server that only runs your stuff) or a shared instance. MyClawGo provides dedicated per-user instances. Some cheaper services use shared infrastructure.

**What if I want to test managed hosting before committing?**  
Most managed OpenClaw hosting providers offer monthly billing with no long-term commitment, so you can try for one month and cancel if it's not worth it.
